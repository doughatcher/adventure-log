title: The Adventure Begins

The vessel cut through the last familiar waters as dusk settled across the open sea, and then — gradually, unmistakably — the character of the ocean changed. The waves did not roughen, nor did the wind shift against them, yet something older and heavier moved in the currents beneath the hull. It was Vargr who felt it first, his great hands resting against the rail, his eyes going distant the way they did when the spirits pressed close. He said nothing for a long moment, only watched the water darken in ways that had nothing to do with the fading light.

Before them, vast and patient as a sleeping throne, the Shard C — the Sea of Governance — opened wide. The air above it tasted of ink and iron, of sealed documents and old authority. Where the Wild Reaches had pressed upon them with the weight of living things, and the Corrupted Depths had whispered against the soul like a fraying thread, this place pressed differently: a bureaucratic gravity, the sense of a ledger being balanced somewhere far below the surface. The party stood at the prow together and felt, each in their own way, that this sea was watching them with something close to deliberate intelligence.

Granit planted both boots against the deck as if reminding the ship itself who was aboard. Rides the Wake stood loose and ready, her stillness the specific stillness of a blade not yet drawn. Lyvriele observed the horizon the way she observed everything — committing it to memory before it had the chance to change. They had cleared the wild and walked out of the corruption. They had earned the right to be here. Whether this place agreed remained to be seen.

They made their anchorage as full dark settled, the energy of the Shard thrumming beneath the waterline in slow, enormous pulses. There were supplies to account for and charts to study, edges to put back on blades that had seen enough work across two shards to need attention. The practical work of survival did not pause for significance. But even as they moved through the familiar rhythms of rest and preparation, each of them understood, without needing to name it, that the world's trouble was changing in nature. Shard by shard, the pattern was becoming legible — and what governed these waters, what had always governed them, had not yet shown its face.

Sleep came. The sea continued its slow, ancient turning around them, patient with the certainty of institutions that have outlasted every crisis that ever threatened to end them. Morning would bring questions that had no easy answers. But the party had not come this far by flinching from what a place meant.

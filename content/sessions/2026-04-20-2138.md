The vessel cut its way into the waters of Shard C as the last light of evening smeared itself across the horizon, and the change in the sea was immediate, unmistakable. Something older than wind moved beneath the hull — slow, deliberate currents that did not answer to tide or storm but to something else entirely, something that had been here long before the first ship ever dared these waters. Rides the Wake stood at the prow and said nothing, which meant she felt it too. The others gathered without being asked.

Granit set a broad hand against the ship's rail and held it there, reading the water the way a physician reads a pulse. The divine current that ran through the dwarf like a vein of ore through granite — steady, deep, unhurried — seemed to quicken, just slightly, as if recognizing something in the ambient power around them. The Sea of Governance had a weight to it that the previous shards had not. Shard A had been wild, hungry, alive in the way of things that have never been named. Shard B had been rot, a wound in the world's fabric. This was different. This was the sea as an argument — as a claim over civilization itself.

Lyvriele moved to the stern and studied the wake the ship left behind them, already smoothing over as though the water did not wish to remember their passage. The Eladrin's gaze carried the particular stillness of someone committing details to memory before they shift. The distant shape of Shard C's waters was vast and grey-green in the failing light, and somewhere beneath the surface those ancient energies circulated like the slow deliberations of a court that has been in session for centuries. Whatever waited here, it would not rush them. It had time.

Vargr Stormcaller spoke last, because the Goliath always waited to hear what the silence had to say before filling it. What he said was quiet enough that only the wind carried it further than a few paces — something about patterns, about escalation, about the way the shards they had faced each time had sharpened the threat rather than repeating it. He had spoken to things that lingered in the last two places. He suspected the things that lingered here would speak a different language altogether. The language of authority. Of law. Of whoever decides who is allowed to sail these waters and who disappears beneath them.

They made for port with instruments checked and blades close at hand, the crew moving with the careful efficiency of people who had learned to read their ship's passengers. There was work ahead — rations to lay in, charts of these waters to find and study, gear to sharpen and attune — the thousand small preparations that stand between a party's survival and the assumption of it. But for a moment, as the Sea of Governance spread before them in all its strange and institutional menace, the four of them simply stood on the deck and let it be what it was. The next trial had a name. Soon enough, it would show them its face.
